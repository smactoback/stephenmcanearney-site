Most arguments about where to build a hardware company start and end with the
usual coordinates: a coast in California, a corridor in the north-east, maybe
Shenzhen if the bill of materials demands it. That map is a century old and it
quietly assumes the constraints of a software business. Frontier hardware does
not have software's constraints, and once you take that seriously the map looks
different.

## The argument in one line

The thesis is simple to state and harder to live by: the inputs that frontier
hardware increasingly competes on — energy, materials, space, and skilled
hands — are not evenly distributed, and one underweighted region has a genuine
surplus of all four.

> The cheapest electron and the rarest mineral rarely live next to the best
> designer. When they do, something interesting happens.

A few things follow from taking that seriously:

- Capital expense, not headcount, dominates the early burn.
- Proximity to industrial offtake shortens the loop from prototype to revenue.
- Talent that has shipped real plants is worth more than talent that has shipped
  real apps.

Each of those deserves its own essay. For now it is enough to notice that none
of them point back to the old map.
