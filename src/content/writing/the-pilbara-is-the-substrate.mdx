Every conversation about Australian AI sovereignty that does not start with the Pilbara is having the wrong conversation.<Sidenote n={1}>Including, with great regret, several conversations I have had in the past month with people whose names appear on government letterhead.</Sidenote> The state of Western Australia exports, in a single bad week, more iron ore by mass than the United States exports of every other physical good combined.<Sidenote n={2}>Australian Bureau of Statistics, <em>International Trade in Goods</em>, March 2026 release. Iron ore: 95.4 Mt. US non-ore exports for the same week: ~94 Mt aggregate, per Census Bureau monthly trade.</Sidenote> The continent is the substrate on which the next two decades of compute will be built — not metaphorically, not as branding, but as a literal accounting fact of where the atoms come from.

I want to make a narrow argument in this essay, and then a broader one. The narrow argument is that the seventeen-layer stack I keep returning to in this publication has, at its base, exactly three layers that are immobile on the relevant timescale: *extraction*, *refining*, and *generation*. Those three are conditioned on geology and climate. Everything above them — fabs, datacenters, models, agents — is portable on a five-year horizon. The bottom three are not.

The broader argument is that *Australia, having missed the software wave for boring and well-documented structural reasons, has not missed this one yet*, and that the window in which a serious industrial policy can land is roughly the lifetime of one Federal Parliament.

## The geography is the moat

Consider what is actually moving when a hyperscaler announces a new region. The buildings are not moving. The fibre is not moving. The grid connection — the part that matters — was negotiated three years before the press release.<Sidenote n={3}>The PJM interconnection queue is presently quoting <em>seven years</em> for a new gigawatt-class load. Source: PJM Interconnection Queue, Q1 2026.</Sidenote> What is moving is a contract: a promise to consume electricity at a known schedule, at a known price, for a known duration. Hyperscalers are, on a long enough horizon, electricity buyers with a side business in software.

The Pilbara has more sun than the Atacama, more wind than the North Sea, and an existing 12-gigawatt mining grid that was built to electrify haul trucks and crushers. It also has, in iron ore royalties, a sovereign income stream large enough to capitalise the transmission infrastructure that the United States is presently failing to build.<Sidenote n={4}>See Bent Flyvbjerg's work on transmission megaprojects; the cost-overrun distribution for HVDC lines is one of the few in infrastructure that has been getting <em>better</em> over the last decade. The math, in other words, has flipped.</Sidenote> None of these facts are controversial. The question is whether anyone in Canberra is going to put them in the same memo.

## What the autonomous truck means

In February I spent three days at the West Angelas operation, watching seventy-eight Caterpillar 793Fs work without humans in the cab.<Sidenote n={5}>Caterpillar 793F, payload 240 t, autonomous since 2008 at Rio's West Angelas pit. The fleet runs ~23 hours/day at 47°C in summer. There is no driver's seat.</Sidenote> I wrote about it at length in № 012 and will not repeat the field notes here. But there is a point worth restating, because it is consistently misunderstood by software people: the autonomous mining fleet is not a preview of physical AI. It is the deployed product. It has been operating, profitably, for more than a decade. Rio Tinto's autonomous trains have run more tonne-kilometres of revenue freight than every robotaxi project, combined, has run passenger-kilometres.

The implication is that the seventeenth layer of the stack — the autonomy layer — already has an Australian incumbent, and it is the iron ore majors.<Sidenote n={6}>Specifically: Rio Tinto, BHP, and Fortescue, in approximately that order by autonomous-fleet maturity. Hancock and Mineral Resources are catching up.</Sidenote> They will not look like AI companies because they do not call themselves AI companies. They will compound at fifteen per cent a year and accumulate the operational data that nobody in San Francisco can buy.

## A note on what to do

I am writing a longer memo on the policy specifics — it will appear here as № 010 was an early sketch of — but the headline is short enough to fit on one notecard. Build the transmission. Permit the gigawatt-scale solar. Buy the grid batteries from whichever Korean firm will sell them to you fastest. Then, and only then, talk about sovereign foundation models.

The bottom three layers are the constraint. Everything else is downstream of them.
